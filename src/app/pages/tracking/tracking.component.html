Emissions from the burning of fuels in stationary sources owned or controlled by an organization. These sources include equipment and systems that are fixed in place, such as boilers, furnaces, turbines, and other types of heating and power-generating equipment.

  1. Liquid fuels: Emissions resulting from the combustion of liquid fuels in stationary sources are a significant part of an organization’s overall carbon footprint due to the widespread use of liquid fuels such as gasoline, diesel, fuel oil, kerosene, and others.
  2. Solid fuels: These fuels typically include coal, peat, and other solid materials used for heating, power generation, or industrial processes.
  3. Gaseous fuels: These fuels are typically in a gaseous state at normal atmospheric conditions and include natural gas, propane, butane, and other similar gases used for heating, power generation, and transportation.
  4. Biofuels: Biofuels are derived from renewable biomass sources such as plant matter, agricultural residues, or waste materials.
  5. Biomass: Biomass refers to organic materials derived from plants or animals that can be used as fuel. This includes wood, agricultural residues, animal waste, and dedicated energy crops.
  6. Biogas: Biogas is a renewable energy source produced through the anaerobic digestion or fermentation of organic materials such as agricultural residues, food waste, sewage sludge, or energy crops.
To collect stationary combustion data for emissions in a company, follow these steps:
  1. Identify stationary combustion sources - Determine which equipment and facilities generate emissions, such as: Boilers, Furnaces, Generators, Heaters, Chillers
  2. Collect fuel consumption data: Fuel Purchase Records - Collect data from fuel purchase receipts and invoices. Usage Logs - Maintain logs of fuel usage in vehicles, machinery, and equipment. Meter Readings - Use meter readings for equipment that consumes liquid fuels. Operational Records - Document hours of operation for fuel-consuming equipment
  3. Relevant departments for collecting the data - Production department, Operational department
How to use the tool for entering data emissions
  1. Select the months and year for which you want to enter the emissions data. If you select multiple months, same data will be replicated for the months selected.
  2. Once submitted, data is sent for approval. Manager of facility or the admin has approval rights. Only the approved data is included in emissions calculations. You can check the approval status in ‘Status’ tab
  3. User can attach the bills, invoices as attachment for record purpose.
Guidance on input fields

Blend type – This is the blend% of biofuel (bioethanol / biodiesel). Final emission factor is the weighted average of emission factor for 100% mineral petrol / diesel and biofuel. Average blend type assumes 7% blend perc.

Calorific Value – If Gross CV of the fuel is entered, it overwrites the average CV.

Emissions from the use and leakage of refrigerants from equipment owned or controlled by an organization. These emissions are significant because many refrigerants have a high global warming potential (GWP), meaning they can trap much more heat in the atmosphere than carbon dioxide (CO2). Common agents include HVAC systems, refrigeration units, and vehicle air conditioning systems.

To collect refrigerants data for emissions in a company, follow these steps:
  1. Inventory of Equipment - List all the equipment that uses refrigerants (e.g., HVAC systems, refrigeration units).
  2. Type of Refrigerant Used - Collect information on the specific types of refrigerants used in each piece of equipment.
  3. Quantity of Refrigerant - Amount of refrigerant in each piece of equipment and total quantities used, including any additions or top-ups.
  4. Leakage and Emissions Data - Records of refrigerant leaks, repairs, and the quantity of refrigerants emitted during maintenance or due to leaks.
  5. Purchase and Disposal Records - Data on the purchase of new refrigerants and the disposal of old or unused refrigerants
  6. Relevant departments for collecting the data - Environment, Health, and Safety/ Facilities Management department

Emissions associated with the generation of purchased electricity used by a company. These emissions are indirect, as they occur outside of the company's direct operations, but are still related to its activities.

This category includes all upstream (i.e., cradle-to-gate) emissions from the production of products purchased or acquired by the reporting company in the reporting year. Products include both goods (tangible products) and services (intangible products).

Purchased goods can be further classified into:
  1. Standard goods – Typically have shorter lifespan. They are directly related to making the final goods (such as intermediate goods) or are non-product related inputs (such as office supplies or mfg. spare parts). The emissions are mainly associated with production, transportation, and disposal. E.g. building material, electronics, metal, plastic
  2. Capital goods – These are final products that have a longer lifespan (e.g. buildings, machinery, vehicles) and are used to produce goods, provide service, or sell merchandise. The emissions are primarily associated with production, use and maintenance.
To collect emissions data for purchase goods and services used by the company, follow these steps:
  1. Identify list of all purchased items: Create a comprehensive inventory of all goods and services procured by the company.
  2. Categorize purchases: Group items into categories (e.g., raw materials, components, office supplies, professional services) or based on their product code.
  3. Try to collect the purchased amount data as much as possible in quantity, this gives more accurate emissions results than spend based data.
  4. Supplier Identification: Identify key suppliers for each category and map out their supply chains.
  5. Data Requests: Request specific emissions data from suppliers, including energy consumption, raw material usage, and transportation details.
  6. Relevant department for collecting the data: Procurement / Supply chain department, Finance/Accounting Department, Logistics Department
How to use the tool for entering emissions data
  1. Select the months and year for which you want to enter the emissions data. If you select multiple months, same data will be replicated for the months selected.
  2. Select the product type either based on category or product code (HSN, ISIC, SAC etc). For entering the data in bulk, download the attached template and fill in the details.
  3. In the vendor section, add the details of the supplier and the specific emissions factors in KgCO2e/unit, if available.
  4. Once submitted, data is sent for approval. Manager of facility or the admin has approval rights. Only the approved data is included in emissions calculations. You can check the approval status in ‘Status’ tab

Bulk upload - Use the Bulk Upload option to quickly enter multiple records

Steps to To enter data in bulk:

  1. Download the bulk upload template from the tool.
  2. Fill in the required fields: product category, description, purchase date, quantity, vendor details, and vendor emission factors (if available).
  3. Click on ‘Select file to upload’ and choose the completed template.

There are three possible outcomes when the tool maps product categories to product descriptions:

  • Matched – Category is correctly linked to the description
  • Unmatched – No category is assigned
  • Matched / other category– Scope 3 category, other than purchased goods & services and capital goods is assigned (e.g., business travel)

Only the matched items go for emission calculations.

Tool uses fuzzy search logic to match the entries with database categories. In case of unmatched entries or matched entries where the user is unsatisfied with the match (as the match is not always 100% accurate), they can do manual matching by clicking on the blue ‘action’ button. If there is no match found in the database, the status of the matched item can be changed to unmatched.

For matched/other category entries, if the user wants to include it in the purchased goods & services category, they can change the status to matched which makes it a part of the matched list used for emission calculations.

In case of unmatched entries, users can use the AI Search to automatically assign correct categories. Steps for AI Run:

  1. Tap on the ‘Run AI Match’ button.
  2. A new window will open where the AI will begin matching product categories to the descriptions.
  3. Monitor the AI match progress; click the ‘Refresh’ button to check the latest status.
  4. Once the batch process is complete, tap ‘Upload Data’ to import the matched data into the existing list.
  5. The AI Run window is user-specific —only the initiating user can access and operate it.

The final list of matched entries (the matched items from manual match plus the ones mapped with AI) will used for emission calculation.

Emissions from the transportation and distribution of products purchased by the company, from suppliers to the company’s facilities. This includes transportation by land, air, and sea, as well as warehousing and storage. Emissions from the transportation and facilities leased by the company are not included in this category.

Examples of upstream transportation and distribution emissions include:
  1. Fuel combustion from trucks, ships, and airplanes transporting goods
  2. Emissions from transportation infrastructure, such as roads and ports
  3. Energy consumption from warehousing and storage facilities
  4. Emissions from packaging materials and labelling
To collect emissions data for Upstream transportation and distribution used by the company, follow these steps:
  1. Methods used to collect the data could be of two types: a) Activity-based approach - track the distance, mode, and fuel type of transportation (more accurate), b) Spend-based approach - estimate emissions based on procurement spend data
  2. Vehicle Suppliers - Contact your suppliers to gather data on their transportation methods and emissions.
  3. Logistics Providers - Obtain emissions data from third-party logistics (3PL) providers or carriers.
  4. Internal Records - Utilize internal records of transportation activities, including type of fuel consumption and vehicle types.
  5. Relevant department for collecting the data – Logistics / Supply chain department, Procurement department
How to use the tool for entering emissions data
  1. Select the months and year for which you want to enter the emissions data. If you select multiple months, same data will be replicated for the months selected
  2. For Vehicles
    • Select vehicle type from the dropdown, also sub-category of the vehicle based on the fuel type.
    • Enter the mass of products that were transported.
    • Enter the distance travelled by the product and the number of vehicles used for transportation.
  3. For Storage Facility
    • Select the storage facility type from the dropdown.
    • Enter the area occupied by the facility in sq ft or sq. meter.
    • Enter the number of days of storage of the product.

Emissions from the disposal and treatment of waste generated in the company’s operations. This includes waste from manufacturing processes, office operations, and other activities.

The different types of waste treatment methods are:
  1. Landfilling – Final placement of the waste into or onto the land in a controlled way
  2. Incineration – Burning waste at high temp. to reduce volume and produce energy
  3. Composting – Breaking down organic waste (vegetables, rotten food, garden waste) into nutrient-rich-soil.
  4. Anaerobic digestion - Microbial breakdown of organic waste to produce biogas.
  5. Recycling - Processing waste materials into new products. It can be open-loop (recycling of material into other products) or closed-loop (recycling back into the same product).

Waste diversion rate – It represents how much of waste you divert from your facility without burn (incineration) or by (landfill). It is calculated as (weight of waste treated by recycling + reuse + composting / total waste weight) %.

To collect emissions data for waste generated in operations used by the company, follow these steps
  1. Identify Waste Streams: Catalogue all types of waste produced by the company's operations, including solid waste, liquid waste, hazardous waste, and recyclable materials.
  2. Determine Waste Sources: Identify the sources of waste generation within the company's operations, such as production lines, offices, cafeterias, and warehouses.
  3. Quantify Waste: Measure the amount of waste generated from each source. This can be done by weighing the waste or using volume estimates. Ensure measurements are taken consistently and regularly.
  4. Classify Waste: Classify the waste according to its type (e.g., organic, plastic, metal, paper) and its disposal method (e.g., landfill, incineration, recycling).
  5. Collect data on Disposal Methods: Gather information on how the waste is disposed of, including the method of disposal, transportation details, and the final disposal site.
How to use the tool for entering emissions data
  1. Select the months and year for which you want to enter the waste items data. If you select multiple months, same data will be replicated for the months selected.
  2. Select the product and the sub-category of product generated as waste from the dropdown.
  3. Select the type of waste disposal.
  4. Enter the amount of waste generated in kg or litres or tonnes.
Water Supply and Treatment

Emissions associated with water withdrawn from the source and transported to the point of use and from water treatment processes.

Total water consumed = water withdrawn – water discharged

Water goes through treatment process before it is discharged. There are three stages of water treatment:

  1. Primary - Removes large objects, debris, and solids from wastewater
  2. Secondary - Removes dissolved and suspended biological matter, often using microorganisms in a controlled environment
  3. Composting – Breaking down organic waste (vegetables, rotten food, garden waste) into nutrient-rich-soil.
  4. Tertiary – This is the last stage and is employed when specific wastewater constituents which cannot be removed by secondary treatment must be removed.
To collect emissions data for water withdrawn and treatment, follow these steps:
  1. Determine the volume of water withdrawn from various sources (e.g., rivers, lakes, groundwater) and discharged back to different destinations.
  2. Estimate the volume of water discharged to various destinations undergoing treatment process. Identify the stages of treatment process applied.
  3. Department responsible for collection water data – Utilities / Operations department, Resource management / Maintenance department
How to use the tool for entering water emissions data
  1. Select the months and year for which you want to enter the water data. If you select multiple months, same data will be replicated for the months selected.
  2. Enter the total water withdrawn and discharged volume in kilo litre or cubic metre.
  3. For water withdrawn by source, input the % breakdown of water supplied from various sources. Similarly, input the % breakdown of water discharged to various destination
  4. Input the % breakdown of water discharged to various destinations which has undergone the treatment process and select the level of treatment done. E.g. total water discharged into surface water undergoing treatment = Total water discharged x a% discharged to surface water x b % of discharged to surface water which has been treated
Business Travel

Emissions from the transportation of employees for business-related activities. This includes travel by air, rail, bus, car, and other modes of transportation.

To collect emissions data for business travel used by the company, follow these steps:

  3. Collect Fuel Consumption Data - Obtain fuel consumption details for company-owned or rented vehicles.
  4. For personal vehicles used for business travel, gather mileage reimbursement records.
How to use the tool for entering data emissions
  1. Select the months and year for which you want to enter the travel data. If you select multiple months, same data will be replicated for the months selected
  2. Select the business travel type such as via flight or other ways of transport and/or hotel stay.
  3. Flight travel – There are three ways of entering flight travel data; Generic type – select the flight type based on travel time, To/Fro – this allows user to enter the take-off and landing ports and is the most accurate method, Distance – when the flight air distance is available

Emissions from the transportation of employees between their homes and workplaces. This includes emissions from personal vehicles, public transportation, etc.

To collect emissions data for employee commute used by the company, follow these steps:
  1. Determine which employees and types of commutes are included.
  2. Collect information on commute patterns, such as:
    • Mode of transportation (car, bike, public transit, walking, etc.)
    • Average distance of daily commute (one-way)
    • Frequency of commute (days per week)
How to use the tool for entering data emissions
  1. Select the year for which you want to enter the commute data. Note – the data is only entered once for the entire year.
  2. Enter the total number of employees commuting to office and number of working days during the month. This data is used to calculate the total commutes for the year - no of employees x no of months x working days per month x 2 commutes per day
  3. In the table, enter percentage details of the total commutes to office against the type of transport. Also, enter the average commute distance in km (one-way).

Emissions associated with company staff working from home, primarily due to energy consumption in heating, cooling, lighting, usage of computer devices and internet.

Three cases have been covered in this category -
  1. With cooling – Summer months for which staff is working from home with the usage of air conditioning (along with office devices)
  2. With heating – Winter months for which staff is working from home with the usage of heating devices (along with office devices).
  3. No heating / cooling – Months for which staff is working from home with only office devices (computer and internet) contributing to emissions and no usage of heating / cooling.
To collect emissions data for home office work, follow these steps:
  1. Setup surveys or questionnaires for home office work. Select sample set of employees working in different location to get a good estimate of the total work from home staff.
  2. Manual data tracking (i.e. spreadsheets / journals) to maintain the data of home office work
  3. Department responsible for collecting the data – Human resources department
How to use the tool for entering data emissions
  1. Select the year for which you want to enter the data. Note – the data is only entered once for the entire year.
  2. Enter the no. of employees working from home in the three different setups.
  3. Enter the average no. of days per week and the no. of months each year for a certain work from home setup.

Processing of Sold products

Emissions from the processing of intermediate products sold by the company. This includes emissions from further manufacturing or assembly of the company’s products by other entities before use by the end consumer.

To collect emissions data for Processing of Sold products, follow these steps:
  1. List all the intermediate products that your company sells.
  2. Use one of these methods to collect the data w.r.t processing of intermediate goods:
    • Site specific method - collect either site-specific GHG emissions data provided by downstream value chain partners or site-specific activity data from downstream processes.
    • Average data method - collect data on the type of downstream process(es) involved in transforming or processing sold intermediate products into final products. It takes industry average emission factors to estimate the final emissions and is relatively less accurate than the first method.

Use of Sold products

Emissions from the use of products sold by the company. This category is particularly significant for companies that produce goods with significant emissions during their use phase, such as automobiles, appliances, and electronics. It includes the total expected lifetime emissions from all relevant products sold in the reporting year across the company’s product portfolio.

We divide emissions from the use of sold products in two types:
  1. Direct use-phase emissions – includes products which directly consume energy (fuel or electricity) during use like automobiles, engines, electronics. Fuels and feedstocks, products emitting GHGs also come under this category
  2. Indirect use-phase emissions – products which indirectly consume energy. Eg apparel (require washing and drying), food (requires cooking and refrigeration), soaps (require heated water)
To collect emissions data for use of products sold by the company, follow these steps:
  1. List all the products sold by the company that have a significant use-phase emissions profile.
  2. Identify the quantity of product sold
  3. Collect the data on lifetime expected use of product, fuel / electricity consumed per usage and refrigerant leakage per use of the product
How to use the tool for entering data emissions
  1. Select the type of energy consumption and product category from the dropdown.
  2. Enter the amount or number of products sold in tonnes/Kg/litres.
  3. If unit selected is ‘no. of units’, enter the fuel / refrigerant data per usage of the product.
End-of-Life Treatment of Sold Products

Emissions from the disposal and treatment of products sold by the company at the end of their life. This includes recycling, landfilling, incineration, and other waste treatment processes.

The emissions from downstream end-of-life treatment of sold products follows the calculation methods in category ‘waste generated in operations’, with the difference that instead of collecting data on total mass of waste generated in operations, companies should collect data on total mass of sold products (and packaging) from the point of sale by the reporting company through the end of life after use by consumers.

To collect emissions data for End-of-life (EoL) treatment of the products sold by company, follow these steps:
  1. List all product categories that the company sells and determine which are relevant for EoL analysis.
  2. Determine the various EoL scenarios for each product category, such as recycling, incineration, landfilling, or composting.
  3. Gather data on the volume or weight of products that have reached the end of their life for each scenario.
How to use the tool for entering data emissions
  1. Select the type of waste generated and the product from dropdown list
  2. Enter the amount of total waste generated from sold goods. In many cases, total waste will be equal to the total products sold in reporting year. However, if the product is actually consumed (e.g., food and drink) the total waste is likely to be lower, and in other cases, such as products combusted to generate energy, could even be zero
  3. Enter the percentage of waste going to Landfill, Combustion, Recycling and Composting.

Franchise

Emissions from the operations of franchises owned by the company who grants the license to other entities to sell or distribute its product or service. This includes all emissions resulting from the franchise operations, such as energy use, transportation, and waste.

To collect emissions data for Franchise used by the company, follow these steps:
